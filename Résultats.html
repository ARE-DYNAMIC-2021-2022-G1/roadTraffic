- First, consider our 'ville_1', traveled by 30 cars (50% electric, 50% thermal)

We notice first of all, according to the graphic representation and the histogram below, that the most traveled roads are the axis (8-9) and (9-10).

Indeed these are the main axes within our city and this is true in real life.

Let’s now apply our different experiments:
Experiment 1 - We take the original city, we remove the busiest road(s) and we get 10 new cities, we then look at the city model with the least C02 emissions.

By running our road model on each of the new cities and comparing the rate of C02 emitted by each of the cities, we get the graph below. It is normal that cities 1,4,5 and 6 have the same C02 emission because it is actually the same city. Indeed for this first example we have chosen a very minimalist city model with only about ten roads (see next model with the simplified example of the city of Paris.

We can see after this first simulation that the original city comes back almost all the time as the one with the least emissions of C02 so it is not worth removing roads in a city for ecological measures.

Experiment 2 - We modify the probability of taking the shortest path.

We create a list of populations in which we modify the percentage to choose the shortest path. It varies between 0.1 and 1 with a step of 0.1 to each list. Then, we look at the C02 emissions for each population: here is the graph obtained. Population 1 being the one with the least chance of getting the shortest path and population 10 is the one where we always take the shortest path. Precision in this minimalist model, the last three populations of (7 to 10) are the same. The result below is therefore consistent.

It is concluded that to obtain the lowest possible carbon emission, in the vast majority of cases cars must take the shortest route.
